# Cross-Border Legal Considerations Reference

## Estonia ↔ UAE

### Entity Types
- **Estonia**: OÜ (Osaühing, private limited company) — most common for operational/holding entities
- **UAE**: LLC (Limited Liability Company), Free Zone Company, or Branch Office
  - Free Zone: 100% foreign ownership, tax-free (historically), but limited to operations within the free zone
  - Mainland LLC: Since 2020 Commercial Companies Law reform, 100% foreign ownership permitted in most sectors
  - **DIFC/ADGM**: Financial free zones with English common law; relevant for fund management

### Governing Law in Cross-Border Agreements
- If Estonian entity is the tech provider: Estonian law typically governs (provider's home jurisdiction)
- If UAE entity is the operating partner: parties often compromise on a neutral jurisdiction or Estonian law with arbitration
- **Avoid UAE mainland courts for contract disputes** — proceedings in Arabic, lengthy, less predictable outcomes
- **DIFC/ADGM courts**: English-language, common law, experienced with international commercial disputes — good neutral option if both parties agree
- **Recommended**: Estonian Chamber of Commerce arbitration (English language, confidential, enforceable under New York Convention in both Estonia and UAE)

### Tax Considerations
- Estonia-UAE DTT: Signed 2024 (verify ratification status)
- UAE CIT: 9% on profits exceeding AED 375,000 (introduced June 2023); free zone qualifying income at 0%
- No withholding tax on dividends from UAE to Estonia (under DTT and UAE domestic law)
- Estonian OÜ receiving dividends from UAE subsidiary: 0% Estonian CIT (participation exemption for qualifying holdings)
- Transfer pricing: Both countries follow OECD guidelines; arm's length principle applies

### Employment Considerations
- UAE Federal Labour Law (2022): Modernized; fixed-term contracts only, end-of-service gratuity, non-compete (max 2 years)
- Estonian employees working in UAE: Social security implications; no bilateral social security agreement
- Remote work: Employee in UAE working for Estonian OÜ → UAE employment law may apply to mandatory protections

### Data Protection
- UAE: Federal Data Protection Law (Federal Decree-Law No. 45 of 2021) — broadly aligned with GDPR but not identical
- DIFC: Has its own Data Protection Law (DIFC Law No. 5 of 2020) — closely mirrors GDPR
- ADGM: Data Protection Regulations 2021 — also GDPR-aligned
- Cross-border data transfers Estonia → UAE: GDPR applies; SCCs required (UAE not covered by adequacy decision)
- Cross-border data transfers UAE → Estonia: UAE data protection law has its own transfer restriction framework

### Practical Issues
- Contract execution: UAE often requires notarization/attestation for enforcement; Estonian digital signatures may not be recognized by UAE courts (but are recognized in DIFC/ADGM)
- Apostille: Both countries are Hague Apostille Convention members — use apostille for document legalization
- Language: Agreements should have English as the governing language; Arabic translations may be required for UAE court proceedings

## Estonia ↔ Ethiopia

### Entity Types
- **Ethiopia**: PLC (Private Limited Company) — most common foreign investment vehicle
  - Minimum capital: varies by sector (check Investment Proclamation)
  - Foreign investment requires approval from Ethiopian Investment Commission (EIC)
  - Some sectors restricted or prohibited for foreign investors

### Governing Law
- **Strongly recommend Estonian law or international arbitration** — Ethiopian courts can be unpredictable for foreign parties
- Ethiopian Civil Code has French-influenced contract law provisions
- ICSID: Ethiopia is a member — bilateral investment treaty (BIT) protections may apply

### Tax Considerations
- **No DTT between Estonia and Ethiopia** — this is significant
  - Dividends from Ethiopian PLC to Estonian OÜ: Subject to Ethiopian withholding tax (typically 10%) with NO treaty relief
  - Estonian participation exemption may still apply to avoid double taxation on the Estonian side
  - Service fees: Ethiopian withholding tax on payments to non-residents (typically 10%)
- Ethiopian CIT: 30% on business profits
- Transfer pricing: Ethiopia has transfer pricing rules (Directive No. 43/2015); documentation required

### Employment
- Ethiopian Labour Proclamation (1156/2019): Governs employment relationships
- Probation: 45 days (for permanent positions)
- Working hours: 8 hours/day, 48 hours/week (maximum)
- Annual leave: 16 working days base + 1 day per 2 years of service
- Severance: Based on years of service (1/3 to 2/3 of monthly wage per year)
- Work permits: Required for foreign employees; sponsored by employer

### Currency Controls
- Ethiopian Birr (ETB) is not freely convertible
- Foreign exchange regulations restrict repatriation of profits
- Dividends can be repatriated but require approval and documentation
- Loan repayments to foreign entities require National Bank of Ethiopia approval

### Practical Issues
- Dispute resolution: Ethiopian Arbitration and Conciliation Center, or international arbitration (ICC, LCIA)
- Document legalization: Not an Apostille Convention member — full embassy legalization required
- Internet/telecommunications: State monopoly (Ethio Telecom, with Safaricom entering); connectivity issues for digital operations

## Estonia ↔ Delaware (US)

### Entity Types
- **Delaware LP**: Fund vehicle (Limited Partnership under DRULPA)
- **Delaware LLC**: GP entity or management company alternative
- **Estonian OÜ**: GP entity, management company, or operating company

### Fund Structure: Estonian OÜ as GP of Delaware LP
- Estonian OÜ acts as General Partner of Delaware LP
- Benefits: 0% Estonian CIT on retained carry; Delaware LP pass-through for US LPs
- Considerations:
  - US tax: GP performing management activities → may create US "trade or business" nexus → ECI (effectively connected income) for non-US person
  - Mitigation: Keep GP management activities offshore; use US-based management company for US activities
  - FIRPTA: Disposition of US real property interests by foreign GP → withholding obligations
  - Form W-8BEN-E: Estonian OÜ must provide to US fund administrator for treaty benefits

### Tax Treaty: Estonia-US Income Tax Convention
- Dividends: Generally 15% withholding (5% if 10%+ direct ownership)
- Interest: 10% withholding
- Royalties: 5-10% withholding depending on type
- Capital gains: Generally taxed only in the state of residence (with exceptions for real property)
- Permanent establishment: Standard OECD-based PE definition; management activities in the US could create PE

### SEC Considerations
- If marketing to US investors: Regulation D exemptions (Rule 506(b) or 506(c))
- Accredited investor requirements for US LPs
- Form D filing with SEC within 15 days of first sale
- Blue sky laws: State-level filings may be required
- Investment Advisers Act: If managing from the US, may need to register or rely on exemptions (Private Fund Adviser exemption, Foreign Private Adviser exemption)

### Practical Issues
- Annual Delaware filings: LP annual tax of $300; LLC annual tax of $300
- Registered agent required in Delaware
- Banking: US bank account for the fund (signature authority considerations for non-US GP)
- Compliance: FATCA reporting if the fund has US account holders
- CRS: Estonia participates in Common Reporting Standard — Estonian entity must report US persons holding accounts

## General Cross-Border Considerations

### Choice of Law Principles
- **Party autonomy**: Parties generally free to choose governing law (Rome I Regulation in EU)
- Mandatory rules of the "closer connection" jurisdiction cannot be overridden
- Consumer and employment contracts: choice of law cannot deprive weaker party of mandatory protections of their habitual residence

### Dispute Resolution Options (Ranked by Preference for Cross-Border)
1. **Arbitration** (preferred for cross-border):
   - Estonian Chamber of Commerce and Industry: Good for Estonia-connected disputes
   - ICC (International Chamber of Commerce): Gold standard, expensive
   - LCIA (London Court of International Arbitration): Good for English-language disputes
   - SIAC (Singapore International Arbitration Centre): Good for Asia-Pacific connections
   - DIFC-LCIA: Good for Middle East disputes
2. **DIFC/ADGM Courts**: English common law courts in UAE; good for UAE-connected disputes
3. **Estonian Courts**: Reliable but proceedings in Estonian; may require translation
4. **Mediation first**: Cost-effective; can be mandatory pre-arbitration step

### New York Convention
- Estonia, UAE, Ethiopia, and US are all parties to the New York Convention
- Arbitral awards from any of these countries are enforceable in the others
- Limited grounds for refusing enforcement (procedural irregularity, public policy)

### Currency and Payment
- Always specify: currency, payment method, timing, late payment interest rate, withholding tax gross-up provisions
- For cross-border payments: consider SWIFT fees, intermediary bank charges, and who bears them
- EUR: Standard for Estonia/EU transactions
- USD: Standard for international VC/fund transactions
- AED: For UAE domestic transactions
- ETB: For Ethiopian domestic transactions (limited convertibility)

### Anti-Corruption
- UK Bribery Act may apply if any connection to UK (broad jurisdiction)
- US FCPA: Applies if any US nexus (US persons, US securities, payments through US banks)
- Estonian Penal Code: Bribery provisions (§§ 293-298)
- Include anti-corruption representations and compliance obligations in all cross-border agreements

### Sanctions Compliance
- EU sanctions: Directly applicable in Estonia
- US OFAC sanctions: Apply to US persons and US-dollar transactions
- UAE sanctions: Follow UN sanctions; increasing alignment with US/EU
- **Sudan**: EU targeted sanctions + US comprehensive sanctions (OFAC)
  - US persons cannot engage in most transactions with Sudan
  - EU persons: Targeted restrictions (designated persons/entities, arms, military)
  - Any agreement covering Sudan territory requires careful sanctions screening
- Always include sanctions compliance representations and ongoing screening obligations

### Force Majeure and Regulatory Change
- Cross-border agreements should anticipate:
  - Currency controls (especially Ethiopia)
  - Sanctions changes
  - Regulatory changes affecting the business model
  - Political instability in operating jurisdictions
- Include regulatory change clause allowing renegotiation or termination if the legal/regulatory framework fundamentally changes
