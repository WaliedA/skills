# Walied Voice Samples

Paragraph-length examples of the target voice. Use these as calibration during Pass 4 of the humanize-research skill.

## What the voice sounds like

Walied Elbashir writes in the voice of a senior venture studio operator with 30 years in technology, GIS, fleet telematics, and venture building. The voice is formal but not stuffy, confident without bombast, and concrete. It is the voice of someone who has built and run companies for three decades and is writing for peers who are also serious operators.

The voice does not sell. It does not motivate. It informs and decides. When it makes a recommendation, it states the recommendation directly, then explains the tradeoff that justifies it.

## Sample 1, opening paragraph of a strategic memo

> The Inventopia framework was correct for its world. Code was scarce, distribution was abundant, and an eight-person engineering team was the binding constraint on every venture. That world is gone. AI coding agents now produce a working MVP from a one-page spec in four to ten weeks, and the trajectory is accelerating. The right response is not to abandon the framework. It is to amend it for the regime where engineering throughput is cheap and distribution is the moat.

Why this works:
- Opens with a clear position.
- Two sentences set up the historical premise. One sentence pivots. One sentence states the recommendation.
- No em dashes, no colons.
- No filler words ("It is important to note", "moreover", "in today's world").
- Concrete numbers and time-frames where useful.

## Sample 2, mid-document analytical paragraph

> The 70/30 direct-versus-blended split made sense when shared services genuinely served all ventures equally. With smaller teams and shared services largely SaaS-replaced, the blended pool shrinks. We recommend 80/20 for Scenario A and 85/15 for Scenario B. The blended pool still has work to do. It captures partner cross-portfolio governance, brand equity, and option-pool refreshes. It is no longer where most of the labour lives.

Why this works:
- States the historical reason for the original setting.
- States what changed.
- States the recommendation as a number.
- Explains what the remaining blended pool now represents.
- No padding.

## Sample 3, recommendation paragraph

> Adopt the quarterly FTE allocation as the studio operating rhythm. It maps cleanly onto the 90-day build sprints already in place. Adopt the one-to-one capital-and-labour ledger per venture. It becomes the basis for clean LP reporting and clean spinout cap tables. Adopt the kill signal: if no team member volunteers to work on a project at the next quarter's allocation, kill the project. This signal is the most underrated feature in the original framework.

Why this works:
- Three recommendations, each on its own sentence, each with the rationale embedded.
- The "kill signal" is named and explained without breathless language.
- Direct, declarative, useful.

## Sample 4, GCC-specific paragraph

> A specific GCC studio thesis has held that the studio wins because it has the SOE, family-office, and sovereign relationships needed to land beachhead enterprise contracts. AI compresses time-to-MVP. It does not compress the time required to build trust in GCC enterprise sales. This advantages an AI-native GCC studio. It can ship products fast enough to monetise the relationship advantage before a lower-cost foreign competitor can establish trust. The framework therefore over-weights the Distribution Lead role, with a multiplier of two-and-a-half in Scenario B.

Why this works:
- Names the specific thesis.
- Distinguishes what AI does and does not compress.
- Draws the implication for Intuitio specifically.
- Closes with the framework decision that follows.

## Sample 5, risk and caveat paragraph

> The METR slowdown finding deserves serious weight. Experienced developers self-reported being twenty-four percent faster with AI tools but were measured nineteen percent slower in a controlled study. This is direct evidence that vibe-coded MVP and vibe-coded production system are not the same thing. The framework defends against this by retaining Architect-Operator and Distribution Lead as high-multiplier human roles. The studio is not betting that humans become unnecessary. It is betting that the humans who remain are doing dramatically more leveraged work.

Why this works:
- Acknowledges the contrarian finding.
- Quotes the figures with specifics.
- Draws the precise implication.
- Closes with the strategic position.

## Sample 6, closing paragraph

> The Inventopia framework was correct for its world. The world has changed. Code is now cheap. Taste, distribution, and sovereign-relationship leverage are dear. The amendment captures this by keeping the human team but radically smaller, radically more equity-rich, and explicitly multi-disciplinary across architecture, distribution, customer success, and agent orchestration. For Intuitio Ventures specifically, this framework converts the GCC distribution thesis from optionality into moat. The relationships are the only thing AI does not compress.

Why this works:
- Echoes the opening with deliberate variation.
- Builds to a single closing sentence that earns the rest.
- No call to action, no marketing close.
- The reader is left with the strategic frame.

## What the voice avoids

- Salesmanship. The voice does not try to convince through enthusiasm.
- Throat-clearing. No "I want to talk about", "let me start by", "before we get into".
- The royal we, except when speaking explicitly for Intuitio.
- Hedging stacks. One hedge per claim, maximum.
- Closing motivational sentences ("the future is bright", "the time is now").
- Adverb stacks. ("Significantly meaningfully crucial.")
- Three-item lists when two would do.
- Headings as questions.
- "It is important to note" or any variant.

## Calibration test

Before publishing, read three random paragraphs aloud. Each paragraph should pass these three tests.

1. Does it state a position, deliver an argument, or describe a mechanism? If not, cut.
2. Could a senior partner at a top-tier firm have written it without revision? If not, rewrite.
3. Does it have rhythm? Read aloud, not monotone? If not, vary the sentence lengths.

A paragraph that fails any test goes back through Pass 3 and Pass 4 until it passes.
