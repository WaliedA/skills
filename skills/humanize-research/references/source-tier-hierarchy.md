# Source-Tier Hierarchy

Used during Pass 1 of the humanize-research skill. In Mode D (academic peer-review pass), every source is tagged with a tier marker, and the reference list is audited against target ratios. This file documents the six-tier framework, the rationale, and the target mixes by output type.

## The six tiers

### Tier 1. Peer-reviewed academic publications

Highest evidentiary weight. Includes:

- Journal articles in peer-reviewed venues with editorial board oversight (any indexed in Scopus, Web of Science, ABS or AJG list, FT50, JCR).
- Books from academic publishers (Cambridge UP, Oxford UP, MIT Press, Harvard UP, Routledge academic series, Springer academic, Elsevier academic).
- Edited academic volumes from the same publishers.
- Doctoral dissertations from accredited universities (with the caveat that they are formally less peer-reviewed than journal articles).

Indicators of Tier 1 status. DOI present. Editorial board listed. Manuscript review process documented. Citations from other Tier 1 work.

### Tier 2. Working papers and pre-prints

High evidentiary weight when from established venues, lower when from individual websites.

- NBER (National Bureau of Economic Research) working papers.
- SSRN papers, especially those affiliated with university research centres.
- arXiv preprints in computer science and economics.
- ResearchGate preprints from named scholars.
- Working papers from institutions like CEPR, Bruegel, Brookings.
- TalTech, Sorbonne, and other university research series papers.

Caveat. Preprints have not undergone formal peer review. Use with explicit acknowledgement when load-bearing. Many preprints become Tier 1 publications later. Prefer the published version when available.

### Tier 3. Government, regulatory, and central bank sources

High evidentiary weight for policy and regulatory claims.

- Central bank publications (Federal Reserve, ECB, Bank of England, SAMA, CBUAE).
- Regulatory authority publications (DIFC, ADGM, FSRA, SCA, FCA, SEC, ESCA).
- National statistics offices (UAE Federal Competitiveness and Statistics Authority, GASTAT in Saudi Arabia, Eurostat).
- Government white papers, ministry reports, parliamentary committee reports.
- Multilateral institutions (World Bank, IMF, OECD, UNCTAD, UNDP).
- Sovereign wealth fund disclosures (PIF, ADIA, Mubadala) where formally published.

These sources are typically empirical, well-sourced, and treated as authoritative within their scope. They are not peer-reviewed in the academic sense, but the institutional review process is rigorous.

### Tier 4. Industry research and consulting reports

Useful for market sizing, industry trends, and quantitative benchmarks. Lower evidentiary weight for theoretical claims.

- Top consultancies (McKinsey Global Institute, BCG Henderson Institute, Bain Insights, Deloitte Insights, PwC Strategy&).
- Big Four accountancy firms' research (KPMG, EY, PwC, Deloitte).
- Specialised industry research firms (Gartner, Forrester, IDC, MAGNiTT for MENA, CB Insights, PitchBook).
- VC firm research (a16z, Sequoia, Bessemer, Accel research publications, Tomasz Tunguz at Theory Ventures).
- Industry association reports (CB Insights State of AI, Stack Overflow Developer Survey, Statista).

Caveats. These sources have commercial agendas. Their methodology may not be transparent. Use them for industry signal but not for theoretical foundation.

### Tier 5. Reputable financial and technology news

Useful for current events, recent funding rounds, and stated quotes.

- Financial Times, The Wall Street Journal, The Economist, Reuters, Bloomberg.
- Specialist tech publications. TechCrunch, The Information, Stratechery, Platformer (when by named journalists).
- Business news. CNBC, Fortune, Forbes (when not branded-content), Quartz.
- Regional news with editorial standards. Arab News, Khaleej Times, The National (UAE), Gulf News.

Caveats. News articles report rather than analyse. They are subject to source bias and the speed-versus-accuracy tradeoff. Use them for who-said-what-when and for funding-round figures. Do not use them as substitutes for theoretical sources.

### Tier 6. Blogs, Substacks, social media, and grey literature

Lowest evidentiary weight in academic context. Useful only for documenting emerging trends, capturing direct voice from named individuals, or referencing first-person reports.

- Blogs and Substacks by named individuals (Tomasz Tunguz, Ben Yoskovitz on Focused Chaos, Stratechery before paywall, Lenny's Newsletter, NFX Briefs).
- X/Twitter posts (cite by author, date, and URL; quote precisely).
- LinkedIn posts (similar to X).
- Podcast transcripts and YouTube interviews (when from named interviewees).
- Forum posts (Hacker News, Reddit), only with strong contextual justification.

Caveats. Many Tier 6 sources are valuable, but they cannot be the primary source for an empirical or theoretical claim in a peer-reviewed paper. Use them to capture the contemporary voice and to document arguments being made by practitioners. For load-bearing claims, find a Tier 1 to 4 source for the same point.

## Target ratios by output type

Different venues expect different source mixes. The skill should audit the reference list against these targets.

### Peer-reviewed journal article (top management or IS journals)

| Tier | Target percentage |
|---|---|
| Tier 1 (peer-reviewed) | 50 to 70 percent |
| Tier 2 (working papers, pre-prints) | 10 to 20 percent |
| Tier 3 (government, regulatory) | 5 to 15 percent |
| Tier 4 (industry research) | 5 to 15 percent |
| Tier 5 (news) | 0 to 10 percent |
| Tier 6 (blogs, social) | 0 to 5 percent |

Reviewer expectations. The bulk of the reference list should engage scholarly literature. Industry sources are acceptable for empirical content but should not dominate.

### Conference paper (mid-tier conferences, including WASD)

| Tier | Target percentage |
|---|---|
| Tier 1 | 30 to 50 percent |
| Tier 2 | 10 to 20 percent |
| Tier 3 | 10 to 20 percent |
| Tier 4 | 10 to 20 percent |
| Tier 5 | 5 to 15 percent |
| Tier 6 | 0 to 10 percent |

More flexibility than journal articles. Practitioner-academic conferences accept and expect industry sources alongside academic ones.

### Working paper

Similar to conference paper, with additional flexibility on Tier 5 and Tier 6 sources where the paper is documenting an emerging phenomenon.

### Policy brief or commissioned report

| Tier | Target percentage |
|---|---|
| Tier 1 | 15 to 30 percent |
| Tier 2 | 5 to 15 percent |
| Tier 3 | 30 to 50 percent (high, because policy claims need regulatory grounding) |
| Tier 4 | 10 to 25 percent |
| Tier 5 | 5 to 15 percent |
| Tier 6 | 0 to 10 percent |

Policy briefs lean heavily on Tier 3 sources because policy recommendations require regulatory and institutional grounding. Tier 1 still matters for theoretical framing but is less dominant.

### Thought-leadership piece (LinkedIn essay, blog post, LP whitepaper)

No formal targets. Use sources that genuinely support the claim. The audit is less about ratios and more about avoiding hallucinated or misrepresented sources.

## How to apply the audit

In Mode D, for each citation in the reference list:

1. Tag the source with its tier (1 to 6).
2. Compile a tier-distribution table.
3. Compare to the target ratio for the venue.
4. If the mix falls outside the target range, flag for revision in the Peer-Review Readiness Scorecard (Criterion 7).

### Example tier audit

| Tier | Count | Percentage |
|---|---|---|
| Tier 1, Peer-reviewed | 18 | 36% |
| Tier 2, Working papers | 4 | 8% |
| Tier 3, Government/regulatory | 7 | 14% |
| Tier 4, Industry research | 12 | 24% |
| Tier 5, News | 6 | 12% |
| Tier 6, Blogs/social | 3 | 6% |
| **Total** | **50** | **100%** |

Verdict for journal article submission. Tier 1 at 36 percent is below the 50 to 70 percent target. Recommendation. Add 5 to 8 peer-reviewed sources to lift Tier 1 above 50 percent. Areas to strengthen. The literature review section currently leans on industry research; replace with corresponding peer-reviewed articles where available.

## How to find replacement Tier 1 sources

When the audit finds the Tier 1 share is too low, the skill should help identify peer-reviewed substitutes for industry or news sources. Useful databases.

- Google Scholar (scholar.google.com).
- Scopus (institutional access usually via TalTech or Sorbonne).
- Web of Science.
- ABI/Inform.
- SSRN (for working papers in business and economics).

Search strategy. For each Tier 4 or Tier 5 source making a load-bearing claim, search for academic equivalents using the claim's key terms and adjacent scholar names. Often a peer-reviewed paper has made the same point earlier with stronger methodology.

## Notes on AI tooling sources

The fast-moving AI agent and vibe-coding domain creates a specific tier-mix problem. Many of the most current claims (METR Time Horizon updates, Cursor revenue, Anthropic benchmark scores) have not yet been published in peer-reviewed venues. Tier 4 and Tier 5 sources are inevitable.

Resolution. Use the most authoritative available source. METR's own publications are Tier 2 (research lab pre-prints). CNBC reporting on Cursor's Series D is Tier 5. Anthropic's official benchmark publications are Tier 4 (commercial research, but methodologically transparent).

For these claims, the skill should:
1. Tag honestly (do not inflate Tier 5 to Tier 2).
2. Flag in the manuscript that the cited claim is from current commercial reporting and not yet peer-reviewed.
3. Suggest peer-reviewed framing literature alongside (e.g., theoretical work on labour-substituting technology, productivity measurement, or innovation diffusion).

This is the honest path. Reviewers respect candour about source quality more than they respect inflated tier claims.
