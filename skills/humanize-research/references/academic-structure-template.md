# Academic Structure Template

Used during Pass 5 of the humanize-research skill (Mode D only). This file provides the structural skeletons for the three academic output types Walied is most likely to produce. Apply the appropriate template based on the user's stated venue.

## Template selection

| Venue | Template |
|---|---|
| Peer-reviewed journal article | IMRaD (Section 1) |
| Conference paper (WASD, ICIS, AOM, etc.) | Conference (Section 2) |
| Working paper (SSRN, NBER, TalTech) | Working paper (Section 3) |
| Thesis or dissertation chapter | Thesis chapter (Section 4) |
| Policy brief or commissioned report | Policy brief (Section 5) |

## 1. IMRaD template (peer-reviewed journal article)

Target length 6,000 to 10,000 words including references. Some journals (Strategic Entrepreneurship Journal, Research Policy) accept up to 15,000.

### Section 1.1 Title page

- Title (no colon. "Title. Subtitle" or "Title, Subtitle". Avoid "punny" titles).
- Author names with affiliations and ORCID identifiers.
- Corresponding author email.
- Word count (excluding references and appendices).
- Acknowledgements (brief).
- Funding statement.
- Conflict of interest statement.
- AI use disclosure statement.

### Section 1.2 Abstract

150 to 300 words. Structured into four micro-paragraphs without explicit subheadings, but each paragraph performs one function.

1. Background and motivation (2 to 3 sentences).
2. Methods and data (2 to 3 sentences).
3. Findings (2 to 3 sentences with at least one specific number).
4. Implications and contribution (2 sentences).

### Section 1.3 Keywords

4 to 8 keywords, comma-separated. Should match common indexing taxonomies for the target journal. Avoid being too generic (just "AI" is wasted) or too specific (a single proprietary term is wasted).

### Section 1.4 Introduction (800 to 1,200 words)

Five elements, in order, with no explicit subheadings.

1. The problem or phenomenon. What real-world or theoretical puzzle motivates the paper? One paragraph.
2. The gap. What does the existing literature not yet explain? One paragraph. Cite the most relevant 4 to 8 sources.
3. The research question or questions. State explicitly. Use a question form ("Does X cause Y?") or a hypothesis form ("H1. X causes Y.").
4. The approach. One sentence on what the paper does (theoretical contribution, empirical analysis, case study, etc.).
5. The contribution and roadmap. What does the paper contribute, and how is the rest of the paper organised? Two paragraphs.

### Section 1.5 Literature review (1,000 to 2,000 words)

Synthesis, not listing. Group prior work into thematic strands relevant to the research question. Each strand should:
1. Identify the key papers.
2. State what they collectively argue.
3. Identify the limitation or gap that motivates the present paper.

Avoid the AI-tell "previous studies have shown" structure. Instead, name the scholars and engage with their specific arguments.

End the section with a synthesis paragraph that states what the literature collectively does not yet answer, and why.

### Section 1.6 Theoretical framework or conceptual model (optional, 500 to 1,000 words)

Many management and information-systems journals expect this. Some economics journals do not.

If included, this section presents the conceptual model, defines the constructs, and states the propositions or hypotheses formally.

### Section 1.7 Methodology (1,000 to 2,000 words)

Sub-sections with explicit subheadings. Each sub-section may use a numbered subsection identifier without colons (e.g., "3.1 Research design", not "3.1: Research design").

Standard sub-sections.

- 3.1 Research design (qualitative, quantitative, mixed-methods, case study, design-science, etc.)
- 3.2 Data sources (what data, where from, time period, sampling)
- 3.3 Variables or constructs (with operational definitions)
- 3.4 Analytical approach (how the data is analysed, with citations to the methodological literature)
- 3.5 Validity and reliability checks (or trustworthiness, for qualitative)
- 3.6 Ethical considerations (where applicable)

The methodology should be detailed enough that a reviewer or another researcher could replicate the study.

### Section 1.8 Findings or Analysis (1,500 to 3,000 words)

Lead with the data. Tables and figures appear here. Each table or figure has a numbered caption.

For quantitative work, present descriptive statistics first, then tests of hypotheses, then robustness checks.

For qualitative work, present themes with supporting quotes, organised by research question.

For case studies, present the case narrative followed by analytical observations.

Stay descriptive and analytical. Save interpretation for the discussion section.

### Section 1.9 Discussion (1,000 to 2,000 words)

Three elements.

1. Interpretation. What do the findings mean?
2. Placement in the literature. How do the findings extend, qualify, or challenge prior work? Cite specifically.
3. Engagement with counter-arguments. What is the strongest alternative explanation for the findings, and why is the paper's interpretation more defensible?

This is the most important section for getting through review. Reviewers look here for theoretical contribution and intellectual honesty.

### Section 1.10 Limitations (300 to 600 words)

Distinct section. Not a single sentence buried elsewhere.

Cover at least three categories.

1. Methodological limitations (sample size, data quality, design choices).
2. Scope limitations (what was excluded and why).
3. Generalisability limitations (where might the findings not transfer).

Honest limitations strengthen rather than weaken a paper. Reviewers respect candour.

### Section 1.11 Conclusion (300 to 500 words)

Re-state the contribution in one paragraph. Identify two to four directions for future research. End.

Do not repeat the abstract. Do not write "in conclusion".

### Section 1.12 References

Full bibliography in the chosen citation style. See `citation-styles.md`.

### Section 1.13 Appendices (optional)

Numbered Appendix A, Appendix B, etc. Suitable for detailed data tables, instrument wording, robustness checks, additional case material, or full transcripts of representative interviews.

## 2. Conference paper template

Target length 4,000 to 6,000 words for full papers, 1,500 to 2,500 for short papers, depending on the conference.

Conference papers are typically less rigid than journal articles but should still observe core structure. WASD's Sustainable Development World Conference series accepts both empirical and conceptual papers.

### Section 2.1 Conference paper structure

- Title, authors, affiliations.
- Abstract (150 to 250 words).
- Keywords (4 to 6).
- Introduction (with research question).
- Literature review or theoretical background (compressed; 600 to 1,000 words).
- Methodology (compressed; 400 to 800 words).
- Findings or analysis (1,500 to 2,500 words).
- Discussion (600 to 1,000 words).
- Conclusion (with limitations integrated; 300 to 500 words).
- References.

### Section 2.2 Notes for WASD specifically

- Audience is multi-disciplinary, mixed academic and policy.
- Sustainable development framing is expected. Connect findings to SDG goals where genuine.
- Tables and figures must be camera-ready. Captions numbered.
- Reviewer turnaround is faster than journals; address comments efficiently.

## 3. Working paper template

Target length 8,000 to 15,000 words. Working papers (SSRN, NBER, TalTech research series, ResearchGate preprints) are intermediate between conference papers and journal articles.

Working papers can be more exploratory and longer than journal versions. The structure mirrors IMRaD but with greater latitude in the literature review and the appendix material.

Common practice. Working papers list the version date and version number ("Version 1.2, April 2026") to manage iteration.

## 4. Thesis chapter template

For Walied's PhD pathway (TalTech or Sorbonne VAE), individual chapters typically follow IMRaD with the following adjustments.

- The chapter has a self-contained introduction and conclusion that connects to the broader thesis argument.
- The literature review is more extensive (3,000 to 6,000 words) because it stands alone.
- The methodology section may reference a separate thesis-level methods chapter.
- The chapter contributes one specific argument or empirical investigation to the thesis as a whole.

Most thesis-by-publication models bundle three to five papers (each at journal-article quality) plus framing chapters. Each paper-chapter follows IMRaD.

## 5. Policy brief template

Target length 1,500 to 3,000 words. Used for commissioned work, government briefings, sovereign-investor advisory, ADGM/DIFC submissions.

Structure.

- Executive summary (200 to 400 words, prominent at top).
- Issue framing (300 to 500 words).
- Evidence base (500 to 1,000 words; lighter than academic but with proper citations).
- Recommendations (300 to 600 words; numbered, specific, actionable).
- Implementation considerations (200 to 400 words).
- References (selective; 8 to 20 sources).

Policy briefs prioritise actionability over methodological detail. They still benefit from the citation cross-check and AI-tell removal disciplines from Passes 1 to 4 of the skill.

## Word count budgeting

A rough guide for total target length and section allocations.

| Section | Journal article (8,000 words) | Conference paper (5,000 words) | Working paper (12,000 words) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Abstract | 250 | 200 | 250 |
| Introduction | 1,000 | 600 | 1,500 |
| Literature review | 1,500 | 800 | 2,500 |
| Theoretical framework | 800 | (folded in) | 1,200 |
| Methodology | 1,200 | 600 | 1,500 |
| Findings | 2,000 | 1,500 | 2,500 |
| Discussion | 1,500 | 800 | 2,000 |
| Limitations | 400 | (in conclusion) | 500 |
| Conclusion | 350 | 500 | 500 |
| References | (excluded from word count) | (excluded) | (excluded) |
| **Total body** | **9,000** | **5,000** | **12,500** |

Adjust to journal-specific guidance. Most management journals exclude references, tables, and appendices from the word count. Some include them. Always check author guidelines.

## Tables and figures

Each table or figure needs:
- A number ("Table 1", "Figure 3").
- A short, declarative caption (no colon).
- A source line if data is from a third party.
- A note line for any qualifications or definitions.

Caption format.

- Standard. "Table 1. Descriptive statistics for the studio team sample (N = 47)."
- Walied-adapted. Same. The standard form already uses period rather than colon, which fits the rules.

Reviewers expect tables and figures to stand alone. A reader should be able to understand a table without reading the surrounding prose.
