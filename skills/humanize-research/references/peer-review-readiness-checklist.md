# Peer-Review Readiness Checklist

Used during Pass 6 of the humanize-research skill (Mode D only). The skill produces a Peer-Review Readiness Scorecard appended to the academic output, scoring twelve criteria as Pass, Conditional, or Fail. This file provides the criteria, the scoring rubric, and a Reviewer Response Anticipation template.

## How to use this checklist

1. After Passes 1 to 5 are complete, run through each of the twelve criteria below.
2. Score each as Pass, Conditional (with notes on what would lift it to Pass), or Fail.
3. Compile the scorecard into a single table at the end of the document.
4. For any Fail or Conditional rating, return to the relevant pass and fix.
5. After the scorecard, write the Reviewer Response Anticipation document. Three to five anticipated reviewer objections, each with a one-paragraph response.
6. Do not submit a paper to a peer-reviewed venue until the scorecard shows zero Fails.

## The twelve criteria

### 1. Research question clarity

Is the central research question or hypothesis stated explicitly in the abstract and the introduction?

- Pass. The question is stated as a single sentence in both the abstract and the introduction. It is falsifiable.
- Conditional. The question is implicit but recoverable. The reader can extract it but it is not stated directly.
- Fail. The reader cannot identify what the paper is trying to answer.

### 2. Literature review synthesis

Does the literature review synthesise prior work into thematic strands, and does it identify a specific gap that the paper fills?

- Pass. Three or more thematic strands are clearly identified. Each strand has named scholars and stated arguments. The gap is explicit.
- Conditional. Some synthesis is present but the gap is unclear or oversold.
- Fail. The literature review is a list of papers without synthesis, or the gap is missing.

### 3. Methodology rigour

Is the methodology section detailed enough that another researcher could replicate the study? Are data sources identified, sampling explained, analytical approach justified, and validity addressed?

- Pass. All four sub-elements (data, sample, analysis, validity) are present and detailed. Methodological choices are justified with citations to the methods literature.
- Conditional. Most elements present but one is thin (often validity).
- Fail. The methodology is hand-waved. A reviewer cannot tell what was done or how.

### 4. Findings traceability

For every claim in the findings or analysis section, can the reader trace it back to specific data?

- Pass. Findings are presented with tables, figures, or representative quotes. Each finding cites the underlying data.
- Conditional. Most findings are traceable but some claims are unsupported.
- Fail. The findings section makes claims that cannot be linked to the data presented.

### 5. Counter-argument engagement

Does the discussion engage the strongest alternative explanation for the findings?

- Pass. The discussion section explicitly considers at least one counter-explanation and explains why the paper's interpretation is more defensible.
- Conditional. Counter-arguments are mentioned but dismissed without engagement.
- Fail. The paper presents its findings as the only plausible interpretation. Reviewers will read this as intellectual incuriosity.

### 6. Limitations honesty

Are limitations acknowledged in a distinct section, with at least three categories covered (methodological, scope, generalisability)?

- Pass. Distinct Limitations section, three or more categories, written in plain language.
- Conditional. Limitations are mentioned but in a single sentence elsewhere, or only one category is covered.
- Fail. The paper makes no acknowledgement of limitations, or buries them so as to obscure them.

### 7. Source-tier mix

Does the reference list show appropriate use of peer-reviewed sources?

- Pass. At least 60 percent of citations are Tier 1 (peer-reviewed) or Tier 2 (working papers, regulatory). For business and policy papers, this can drop to 50 percent if industry reports and primary news sources are critical to the empirical content. See `source-tier-hierarchy.md`.
- Conditional. The mix is plausible but light on peer-reviewed sources. Reviewer is likely to ask for stronger theoretical grounding.
- Fail. The reference list is dominated by blogs, news, and Substack posts. The paper looks under-grounded.

### 8. Citation accuracy

Does every cited source genuinely support the claim made in the text?

- Pass. Pass 1 of the skill verified every citation. The Cross-Check Log shows zero remaining unverified or corrected-but-unfixed citations.
- Conditional. Most citations verify but a small number remain unverifiable. They are flagged in the manuscript with caveats.
- Fail. Citations were not verified, or known errors remain in the text.

### 9. Internal consistency

Do the conclusions follow from the findings? Do the findings answer the research question? Are the limitations consistent with the claims made?

- Pass. The argument is internally consistent end to end.
- Conditional. One element is in tension with another but the tension is acknowledged.
- Fail. The conclusions overreach the evidence, or the findings do not answer the research question.

### 10. AI use disclosure

Does the paper include a statement disclosing the use of AI tools in drafting, editing, or analysis?

- Pass. Explicit statement in the manuscript (typically near the acknowledgements). Specifies which tools and for what purposes.
- Conditional. Disclosure is present but vague.
- Fail. No disclosure. As of 2025-2026, this is no longer acceptable at most major journals.

### 11. Conflict of interest and funding

Are conflict of interest and funding statements included?

- Pass. Both statements present. If there is no conflict and no external funding, this is stated explicitly.
- Conditional. One statement is missing or vague.
- Fail. Neither statement is present.

### 12. Plagiarism risk

Does the manuscript contain text that is too close to source material? Does it paraphrase without attribution? Does it self-plagiarise from prior work without disclosure?

- Pass. All paraphrased material is attributed. All direct quotes use quotation marks. Self-citation of prior work is disclosed.
- Conditional. Minor concerns. Some paraphrases run close to original wording but are acceptable.
- Fail. Significant unattributed paraphrase or self-plagiarism. The paper is not safe to submit.

## The scorecard format

Append a table to the academic output with the following format.

| Criterion | Score | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Research question clarity | Pass | Stated in abstract and Section 1.4. |
| 2. Literature review synthesis | Pass | Three strands identified in Section 1.5, gap stated in final paragraph. |
| 3. Methodology rigour | Conditional | Validity sub-section is thin. Recommend adding triangulation note. |
| 4. Findings traceability | Pass | Tables 1 to 4 referenced throughout. |
| 5. Counter-argument engagement | Pass | Section 1.9 considers selection bias as alternative. |
| 6. Limitations honesty | Pass | Distinct Section 1.10, three categories. |
| 7. Source-tier mix | Conditional | 52% Tier 1-2; recommend adding 3 to 5 peer-reviewed citations on AI productivity. |
| 8. Citation accuracy | Pass | All 47 citations verified. See Cross-Check Log. |
| 9. Internal consistency | Pass | Conclusions match findings, limitations match scope. |
| 10. AI use disclosure | Pass | Statement included in acknowledgements. |
| 11. Conflict of interest and funding | Pass | Both stated. Author funded by Intuitio Ventures research budget. |
| 12. Plagiarism risk | Pass | All paraphrases attributed. No self-plagiarism. |

Compile a one-line headline at the top of the scorecard.

> **Headline.** 10 Pass, 2 Conditional, 0 Fail. Recommend addressing both Conditionals before submission.

## Reviewer Response Anticipation template

After the scorecard, write a separate document anticipating the three to five most likely reviewer objections, with one-paragraph responses each.

For each objection.

1. State the objection in the reviewer's likely voice.
2. Score the objection (Major / Moderate / Minor).
3. Identify which section of the paper addresses it (or where it would need to be added).
4. Write the one-paragraph anticipated response.

### Example

**Objection 1. The case-study methodology lacks generalisability beyond the GCC context.**

Score. Major. This is the single most likely critique.

Where addressed. Section 1.10 (Limitations) and Section 1.9 (Discussion).

Anticipated response. The paper does not claim universal generalisability. It claims that GCC venture studios face a specific intersection of conditions (sovereign-LP base, relationship-led B2B sales cycles, common-law DIFC and ADGM jurisdictions for ESOPs) that the existing studio-equity literature does not address. The contribution is therefore contextually-bounded. Future research could test whether the framework adapts to other regional contexts (Southeast Asia, Latin America) where similar structural conditions hold.

### Why this matters

Reviewers respond positively to authors who anticipate criticism. A paper that arrives at review having already engaged the obvious counter-arguments saves the reviewer work and demonstrates the author's intellectual maturity. It also helps the paper get past Reviewer 2.

## A note on Reviewer 2

Every academic submission has a Reviewer 2. The Reviewer 2 is the reviewer who finds the one thing that genuinely needs fixing and the four things that are matters of taste. The Reviewer Response Anticipation document is your insurance against Reviewer 2.

Identify the Reviewer 2 likely lurking in your paper. It is usually a methodological choice or a theoretical commitment that is defensible but contestable. Address it in the Discussion section. Pre-empt the critique. Reviewers respect this.

## Quality threshold for submission

Walied's standing rule. Do not submit until the scorecard shows zero Fails and no more than two Conditionals. If two Conditionals remain, decide consciously whether to address them or to go in. Document the choice in a covering note to co-authors.

For WASD and other practitioner-academic conferences, a slightly looser threshold applies. One Fail in source-tier mix may be acceptable if the paper is empirically novel and grounded in primary data. All other criteria should still pass.

For PhD thesis chapters, the threshold is strict. Zero Fails. Zero Conditionals. Examiner standards exceed reviewer standards.
